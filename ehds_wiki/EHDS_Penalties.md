# EHDS_Penalties

## Definition

The TEHDAS2 M4.1.2 Draft Guideline provides practical support to HDABs in exercising their **supervisory and enforcement powers** under Articles 63 and 64 of the EHDS Regulation. These provisions empower HDABs to monitor compliance, take enforcement actions (revocation, suspension, exclusion), and impose **administrative fines** where appropriate. The enforcement powers complement but do NOT replace the supervisory responsibilities of national Data Protection Authorities under the GDPR.

HDABs are positioned not only as facilitators of secondary data use, but also as **regulators** tasked with ensuring compliance through proportionate and transparent means.

## Supervisory Powers — Article 63

### Article 63(1): Investigative and Monitoring Mandate

HDABs are empowered to request and receive **all information necessary** to verify compliance, including:
- Documentation, records, logs, reports
- Any other material needed to evaluate whether data users or holders fulfil Chapter IV obligations

This authority covers both:
- **Proactive verification** (routine supervisory functions)
- **Reactive investigations** (suspicions, complaints, or alerts from competent authorities, data users, holders, affected individuals, or internal monitoring)

HDABs should establish **standard operating procedures** for audits/inspections with risk-based triggers and escalation criteria. Activities should be documented and insights shared with other national counterparts.

### Article 63(2): Notification and Right to Be Heard

When non-compliance is identified, the HDAB must:
1. **Notify the non-compliant party without delay** — in writing, with evidence, describing specific facts, obligations breached, and contextual information
2. **Grant fair opportunity to respond** — the party must be given a reasonable period, with a **strict upper limit of four weeks**, to present written arguments, factual clarifications, supporting documents, or evidence of remedial actions
3. **Fully consider submissions** before deciding on enforcement measures

If the non-compliance may also constitute a **GDPR breach**, the HDAB must inform the competent supervisory authority without delay and forward all relevant documentation.

### Article 63(3): Enforcement Measures Against Data Users

**Revocation of Data Permit:** Immediate suspension of the user's legal entitlement to access data. The HDAB must:
- Order **immediate cessation** of ongoing processing
- Implement technical enforcement (e.g. disabling SPE access credentials, revoking remote access rights)
- Notify SPE operator and relevant TDHs without delay

**Exclusion from Future Access:** In cases of **serious, repeated, or systemic** non-compliance, the HDAB may exclude the entity from accessing EHDS data for up to **five years**. Must be grounded in national law with a reasoned decision.

**Additional Measures (proportionate and tailored):**
- Require remedial technical/organisational safeguards
- Enhanced oversight
- Formal undertakings not to repeat the breach

### Article 63(4): Enforcement Measures Against Data Holders

When a data holder fails to comply — particularly by **withholding data** or **failing to meet mandatory deadlines** — HDABs may impose:

- **Periodic penalty payments** for each day of delay, when withholding is done with manifest intention to obstruct, or when failing to meet Article 60(2) deadlines
- Must be calculated transparently, proportionate to nature and duration of breach
- For repeated obstruction: escalated enforcement action under national administrative frameworks

### Article 63(7): HealthData@EU IT Tool

The European Commission will define, through implementing acts, a **dedicated IT tool** (part of HealthData@EU infrastructure under Article 75) for HDABs to systematically record and share enforcement measures — including periodic penalty payments, revocations, and exclusions. Until operational, HDABs should ensure their internal systems can capture enforcement information in a structured, transferable format.

### Article 63(8): Future Commission Guidelines

The Commission will issue **detailed guidelines on enforcement measures by 26 March 2032**, in cooperation with the EHDS Board. These will offer interpretive and operational support on periodic penalty payments, revocation/exclusion criteria, and enforcement methodologies.

## Administrative Fines — Article 64

### Article 64(1): General Principles

Fines must be:
- **Effective** — address and correct the specific non-compliance
- **Proportionate** — to the nature, scope, and seriousness of the infringement
- **Dissuasive** — prevent both the offender and others from committing similar breaches

Case-by-case assessment is required, considering the role and responsibilities of the party, risks posed to individuals and the EHDS system, and broader public interest.

### Article 64(2): Criteria for Imposition and Quantification

| Criterion | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Nature, gravity, duration** | How serious the breach is; longer/systemic violations warrant higher fines |
| **Intentionality or negligence** | Deliberate vs. due diligence failure affects culpability level |
| **Mitigation efforts** | Prompt breach reporting, corrective measures, redress offered → possible reduction |
| **Level of cooperation** | Constructive engagement, transparency, willingness to comply → mitigating factor |
| **Financial benefit / cost avoidance** | Unjust enrichment or circumvention of obligations → weighs in favour of stronger sanction |

HDABs must conduct a **holistic, documented analysis** reflected in their reasoning and decision notices.

### Article 64(5): Categorisation of Infringements and Fine Ceilings

The Regulation establishes a **two-tier structure**:

| Category | Description | Maximum Fine |
|----------|-------------|--------------|
| **Less Serious Infringements** | Failure to fulfil procedural/cooperation obligations under Articles 60 (data holders) or 61 (data users) | **€10 million or 2% of worldwide annual turnover** (whichever is higher) |
| **More Serious Infringements** | - Unauthorised processing in breach of data permit terms<br>- Attempts to re-identify individuals (contrary to pseudonymisation/anonymisation safeguards)<br>- Non-compliance with HDAB enforcement measures (persistent/deliberate obstruction) | **€20 million or 4% of worldwide annual turnover** (whichever is higher) |

### Article 64(5): Cumulative Assessment of Multiple Infringements

When multiple infringements occur in relation to the same or linked data permits:
- The **total fine shall not exceed the maximum applicable to the most serious single infringement**
- This prevents disproportionate penalties for closely connected breaches
- HDABs must determine whether breaches form a single continuous infringement or are separate, independent violations

**Repeat or systematic violations** (especially after prior warnings/enforcement) are **aggravating circumstances** → stricter sanctions, higher fines, and additional measures (revocation, exclusion, periodic penalty payments).

### Article 64(7): Procedural Safeguards and Legal Remedies

- Right to be heard: party must be informed of findings and given opportunity to present views (**max 4 weeks**)
- Access to **effective judicial remedies** — challenge fines/decisions before competent national courts
- HDABs must clearly communicate the basis for enforcement, amount/rationale for fines, and available appeal avenues
- Detailed and transparent records must be maintained for accountability, judicial review, and harmonisation

## Implementation Considerations

### Internal Decision-Making Processes
HDABs should develop internal protocols with clear lines of responsibility, documentation standards, and escalation procedures. May draw on national administrative law frameworks and analogous EU enforcement contexts pending EU-level guidance.

### Assessment Tools and Penalty Matrices
HDABs may adopt penalty matrices or risk assessment frameworks to quantify aggravating/mitigating factors and promote consistency. Documentation of how tools are applied enhances traceability, facilitates review/appeals, and supports mutual understanding across Member States.

### Integration with National Legal Frameworks
Each HDAB must align enforcement activities with applicable national law. The EHDS Regulation establishes core enforcement tools, but procedural conditions (time limits for appeal, competent bodies) may vary by Member State — reflecting the principle of procedural autonomy.

### Capacity Building and Training
HDABs need adequate staffing, legal expertise, and technical skills. Specialised training in administrative enforcement procedures, data protection law, and associated legal frameworks is recommended.

## Relationship with GDPR

Enforcement powers under EHDS **complement but do not replace** GDPR supervisory authorities. Where non-compliance involves potential personal data breaches, HDABs must:
- Inform and coordinate with the competent DPA
- Forward all relevant documentation
- Ensure sanctioning activity complies with broader Union data protection principles

## Transparency and Publication

- HDABs must publish **summaries of enforcement measures** on their websites (Article 57(1)(j)(iv))
- Enforcement decisions must be entered into the shared HealthData@EU IT tool for cross-border visibility — within **10 working days** of adoption, with subsequent updates on a **monthly** schedule
- Revocations and exclusions must be systematically notified to other HDABs to **prevent forum shopping**
- Decisions overturned on appeal must be promptly marked as inactive or withdrawn
- When underlying issues are resolved, entries must be updated to reflect closure

### Cross-Border Enforcement Challenges

The M4.1.2 guideline identifies critical unresolved cross-border scenarios:
- If a data user from **Member State A** commits a serious breach while operating inside an SPE in **Member State B**, it is unclear which HDAB holds enforcement competence — both may need to act
- The effect of exclusion decisions (up to 5-year bans) by one HDAB on that user's ability to access data in other Member States remains ambiguous without mutual recognition mechanisms
- Until interoperability rules are established through implementing acts or Commission guidelines, **forum shopping** by non-compliant actors is a material risk
- The guideline calls for eventual alignment through mutual recognition procedures for key enforcement outcomes to ensure credibility and legal coherence of EHDS-wide supervision

## Practical Examples

- **Example 1:** A data user re-identifies individuals from pseudonymised data in an SPE → more serious infringement → fine up to €20M or 4% of turnover + immediate permit revocation + possible 5-year exclusion.
- **Example 2:** A data holder repeatedly fails to provide data within Article 60 deadlines, with evidence of deliberate obstruction → periodic penalty payment per day of delay + escalation to national enforcement proceedings.
- **Example 3:** A user submits a late but complete compliance report (procedural breach under Article 61) → less serious infringement → lower fine range, mitigated by cooperation and absence of harm.
- **Example 4:** Multiple minor procedural breaches across several permits, all linked to the same systemic failure → cumulative assessment → total fine capped at the most serious single infringement maximum.

## Pitfalls

1. **Not coordinating with DPAs:** If an EHDS breach also involves GDPR violations, failure to inform the competent DPA undermines legal coherence.
2. **Mechanical application of fines:** Fines must be individually calibrated — not just based on infringement category but on all Article 64(2) criteria.
3. **Ignoring forum shopping risk:** Failing to notify other HDABs of revocations/exclusions allows non-compliant users to seek access in other Member States.
4. **Inadequate documentation:** Without detailed records of enforcement reasoning, decisions may fail upon judicial review.
5. **Confusing penalty types:** Administrative fines under Article 64 are distinct from national penalty rules under Article 66. Both frameworks must be considered.
